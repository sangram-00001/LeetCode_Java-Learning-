Checking Whether a Number is a Power of a Given Number

When solving problems related to powers of a number, an important concept to remember is that the number should be continuously divisible by the base until the final result becomes 1.

If during the process we obtain a value that is not divisible by the base, or the final result is not equal to 1, then the number is not a power of that base.

1. Power of 2

A number is considered a power of 2 if it can be repeatedly divided by 2 until the final result becomes 1.

Example

16 → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1


Since the number can be divided by 2 four times to reach 1, we conclude:

16=2^4

Thus, 16 is a power of 2.

If the division process results in any value other than 1, the number is not a power of 2.

2. Power of 3

Similarly, a number is a power of 3 if it can be repeatedly divided by 3 until the result becomes 1.

Example

81 → 27 → 9 → 3 → 1

81=3^4

Since the number reaches 1 after repeated division by 3, 81 is a power of 3.

3. Power of 4

A number is a power of 4 if it can be repeatedly divided by 4 until the final result becomes 1.

Example

64 → 16 → 4 → 1

64=4^3

Therefore, 64 is a power of 4.
